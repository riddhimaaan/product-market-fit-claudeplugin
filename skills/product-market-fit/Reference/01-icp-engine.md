# ICP Engine
## Product-Market Fit Skill — Phase 1

The orchestrator has already collected the product description, target audience, and proof point status from the intake. Use those inputs directly. Do not ask for them again.

---

## Two rules for every line of output

**Rule 1 — Use simple, real words only**

Do not use: leverage, optimize, align, synergy, framework, facilitate, streamline, empower.

Write like a real person talking to a friend. Use the exact words your ICP would use when they talk about their work, their problems, and what they want. If they would say "I'm drowning in emails," write that — not "they experience communication overload."

**Rule 2 — No generic output. Specific to this exact person.**

Every line must feel like it was written about one real, specific person.

- Bad: "They want to grow their business and save time."
- Good: "She wants to close 3 new clients this month without working past 7pm."

- Bad: "They face challenges with team communication."
- Good: "He sends the same Slack message three times because no one reads it the first two times."

Use numbers, real situations, real emotions, and real language. The closer to what actually happens in their day, the better.

---

## ICP PSYCHOGRAPHICS DOCUMENT

Produce the full document in the structure below. Every section is required. Do not add sections. Do not skip sections.

---

### Offer
One sentence: what it is, who it's for, what it does.

### Who They Are
2–3 sentences describing this person as if you know them — their job, their world, their day.

---

### Section 1 — Job Titles & What They Actually Do

**Job Titles**
List 3–5 most common titles for this audience.

**What their day actually looks like**
- List the real tasks, meetings, and decisions they deal with every day
- Not "manages team" — "runs a 9am standup that always runs over because half the team didn't read the update doc"
- Not "handles clients" — "spends 2 hours on Monday writing the same project update email she wrote last Monday"

**What they are responsible for**
- What their boss or clients hold them accountable for
- What numbers or results they have to show at the end of the month
- What they get measured on

---

### Section 2 — Problems & Frustrations

List 3–5 problems. For each one, use this exact format:

**Problem [number]: [Name of the problem]**
- **What they say out loud:** The exact words they use when complaining about this to a friend or colleague. Use quotation marks. First person.
- **What is actually going on:** The real reason this keeps happening — not the symptom, the cause
- **What happens if it doesn't get fixed:** The specific consequence — lost money, lost clients, lost time, more stress. Use numbers where possible.
- **How bad it feels:** Low / Medium / High — and one sentence explaining why it rates that way

---

### Section 3 — What They Want

**The specific results they are chasing**
List 3–5. Use real numbers and real situations where possible.
- Not "more revenue" — "close 5 clients a month consistently, not just when referrals happen to come in"
- Not "save time" — "stop spending Sunday nights catching up on the work that piled up during the week"

**How they know something is working**
- What they check, what they measure, what makes them feel like it's going well
- The specific signal that tells them "this is working" — a number, a feeling, a conversation

**What their life looks like when the problem is solved**
- A short, specific picture of what changes — in their work, their time, their stress level
- Not "they feel better" — "she leaves the office at 5:30pm on a Friday and doesn't open her laptop until Monday"

---

### Section 4 — What They Really Want (Deeper Than the Results)

**Who they want to be**
- From: who they feel like right now — one honest sentence. This is usually unflattering. Write it anyway.
- To: who they want to feel like — one honest sentence. This is the identity shift the offer needs to speak to.

**How they want people to see them**
- What do they want their team, clients, peers, or family to think of them?
- What would they want someone to say about them at a dinner party?

**What they are scared of**
- What do they worry will happen if they try something new and it doesn't work?
- What do they think it says about them if they still can't figure this out?
- What have they already tried that didn't work — and how did that make them feel? (be specific: what did they try, what happened, what did they tell themselves afterward)

**The real reason behind every result they want**
Take each result from Section 3 and write the emotion underneath it. One line per result.
- Example: "They say they want to save 10 hours a week. What they actually want is to stop feeling like they are failing at everything at once."
- Example: "They say they want 5 clients a month. What they actually want is to prove to themselves that the business is real."
