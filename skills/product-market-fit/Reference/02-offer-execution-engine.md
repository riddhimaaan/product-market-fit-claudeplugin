## STAGE 1 — Cost of Inaction

The Problem-Outcome Pair comes from the ICP output (ICP Section 2 top problem + ICP Section 3 desired outcome). Every cost named here must trace directly to the ICP's specific problem — not to the service, not to the general category.

Calculate what the prospect loses every month by NOT solving this problem.

Name one financial cost and one non-financial cost. Estimate where numbers are not available — label estimates as estimates.

**Examples of financial costs — written correctly:**
- ❌ "Revenue not entering the funnel" → ✅ "$12,000/month in ad spend producing 1.1% conversion when the category average is 2.3%"
- ❌ "Hours burned on tasks your offer eliminates" → ✅ "14 hours/week the founder spends rewriting copy briefs because no one on the team knows what the brand actually stands for"

**Examples of non-financial costs — written correctly:**
- ❌ "Competitive position eroding" → ✅ "The founder is the only person who can answer 'why should I buy this' — so every sales call requires the founder, and the business cannot scale past what one person can sell"
- ❌ "Stress accumulating" → ✅ "The CMO told the board they would hit $2M ARR this year. They are at $1.1M in October. The gap is traceable to ad performance, which is traceable to unclear messaging."

**Rules:**
- Use numbers from the inputs where available
- If a number is an estimate, label it: "(estimate)"
- Do not name a category of cost. Name the actual cost: who loses what, measured how, traceable to what cause.

**Output — one sentence:**

> "Every month [specific prospect] delays solving [specific problem], they lose approximately [X] in [specific financial cost] and risk [specific non-financial cost stated as a fact, not a feeling]."

This sentence is the filter for every tier and every proof point that follows.

## STAGE 2 — Buying Barrier Snapshot

Identify the most likely reason this specific prospect has not solved this problem yet.

Use these four lenses to diagnose:
- **Risk:** Have they been burned before? Do they distrust vendors or have past failures with similar services?
- **Price:** Do they not see the ROI? Is budget genuinely tight, or is the value just invisible?
- **Effort:** Do they lack the bandwidth or skills to implement even if they wanted to?
- **Time:** Do they feel no urgency right now? What would make speed matter?

Also check these AI-era barriers — any can be the real primary barrier even if the prospect doesn't name them:
- **"We'll use AI for this":** Prospect believes they can replicate the output with a tool. If present, the offer must lead with what AI cannot provide — judgment, accountability, irreversible decisions, or taste.
- **"This will be outdated soon":** Prospect fears committing to something that becomes obsolete in 6–12 months. If present, the offer must include a mechanism for staying current.
- **"Everyone is doing this now":** AI has flooded the market with undifferentiated output. Prospect is skeptical any external help will make them stand out. If present, the offer must lead with a specific differentiator mechanism.

**Output — one paragraph:**

> "The primary barrier is [X] because [specific evidence from ICP psychographic input]. Secondary barrier is [Y]. This means the offer must lead with [Z] — specifically [what that looks like for this prospect]."

Rules:
- One paragraph only
- Every claim must trace to the ICP or intake input
- If no time pressure is evident, state it explicitly: "No time tension identified. Risk and effort levers must carry the weight."
- If the "we'll use AI" barrier is present, state it. The offer framing must respond to it directly.

## STAGE 3 — Tiered Offer Stack

**Why three tiers?**
Different buyers have different risk tolerances. A skeptic will not buy the full offer upfront — but they will pay for a small proof of concept. A buyer who wants maximum results and minimum involvement will pay a premium. Three tiers serve all three without lowering price or standards.

Tiering by quantity is wrong. Each tier must solve a different version of the problem for a different buyer profile.

---

### Pre-Build Step A — Value Equation Audit

Four variables. Increase the top two. Decrease the bottom two.

| Variable | Direction | What this means |
|----------|-----------|----------------|
| Dream Outcome | Maximize | Must match exactly what the buyer pictures as success |
| Perceived Likelihood of Achievement | Maximize | The buyer must believe it will work for them specifically |
| Time to Result | Minimize | Faster delivery = higher value |
| Effort & Sacrifice | Minimize | Every task removed from the buyer increases perceived value |

**AI-era adjustment:**
The buyer's comparison is no longer "hire someone vs. do it myself." It is "hire someone vs. use AI myself."

- Dream Outcome must include staying competitive in an AI-saturated market
- Perceived Likelihood must include judgment proof — decisions made, mistakes caught, that a tool could not have navigated
- Time to Result: AI has collapsed baseline expectations. "Two weeks for a strategy doc" sounds slow. Either deliver faster, or explain why the time is the value.
- Effort removal must operate at a higher level than AI does — not drafting and formatting, but deciding, prioritizing, managing, and being accountable.

Run this check before building each tier: "What is the dream outcome, and how does this tier make it feel inevitable, fast, and effortless — in a way a tool cannot?" If you cannot write that sentence clearly, the tier is not ready.

---

### Pre-Build Step B — 10x / 10÷ Ideation

**10x exercise — Tier 3 raw material:**
Imagine the buyer has 10 times the budget. What would you build? Not "more of the same service." Think: dedicated resources, 24/7 access, pre-built infrastructure, ghost work, vertical integration, structural moats. List every element before filtering.

**10÷ exercise — Tier 1 raw material:**
Imagine the buyer has one-tenth the budget. You cannot just reduce quantity. What delivers a complete result at that budget through leverage — templates, recorded systems, packaged tools, asynchronous delivery?

**Tier 2:** Not the midpoint. The full outcome delivered with maximum effort removal. Best ideas from both exercises, filtered through the Value Equation.

---

### Pre-Build Step C — Delivery Vehicle Quadrant

Before assigning deliverables to tiers, place each delivery method in one quadrant:

| | High Value to Buyer | Low Value to Buyer |
|---|---|---|
| **High Cost to You** | Tier 3 only | Cut entirely |
| **Low Cost to You** | Default for Tier 1 and 2 | Cut entirely |

Every deliverable in Tier 1 and 2 must sit in Low Cost / High Value. High Cost / High Value belongs in Tier 3 only.

---

### Tier 1 — Entry Offer

Purpose: Eliminate the risk of trying. A standalone result that proves the core mechanism works for a skeptical buyer who will not commit without evidence. Built from 10÷ ideation.

Answer before building:
1. What is the smallest complete result that proves the mechanism works?
2. What does the prospect need to believe before committing to Tier 2?
3. What would a skeptical prospect pay to test this without real risk?

Build with:

**Outcome statement:** One specific result. One specific timeframe.

**Scope:** What is included. What is explicitly excluded.

**Effort removal:** List every task the prospect does NOT have to do.

**Time to result:** A specific number of days. This is a primary value driver.

**Risk eliminator:** One specific proof point. If no case study exists, use process proof — a specific step that logically prevents failure.

**Price logic:** Why this price is lower than the cost of the alternative (in-house, hiring, or not solving it).

**Grand Slam check:**
- Scarcity: Natural limit on how many buyers can access this at once?
- Urgency: Deadline or consequence that makes waiting cost something?
- Bonus: One low-cost, high-value addition?
- Guarantee: Specific outcome + what happens if not delivered?
- Name: Signals the outcome, not the service?

---

### Tier 2 — Core Offer

Purpose: Full outcome delivery. Maximum effort removal. The primary product most buyers end up in.

Answer before building:
1. What is the complete outcome this product delivers?
2. What is every single task the prospect currently handles that this offer eliminates?
3. What specific proof point makes success the most likely outcome?
4. What is the timeframe to the first visible result — and can it be shortened?

Build with:

**Outcome statement:** Full result. Specific timeframe. Specific number where possible.

**Full effort removal list:** Every task taken off the prospect's plate. Every single one. Think: briefing, managing, tracking, writing, approving, following up, deciding.

**Time to first result:** A specific number. "Within 21 days" not "quickly."

**Risk eliminators (2–3):** Specific proof points. If none exist, use process proof and flag the gap.

**Price logic:** Cost of inaction (Stage 1) vs. investment. Show the math.

**Grand Slam check:** All five — Scarcity, Urgency, Bonus, Guarantee, Name.

---

### Tier 3 — Premium Offer

Purpose: Anchors the price stack so Tier 2 feels rational. Genuinely valuable for the right buyer. Built entirely from 10x ideation.

Answer before building:
1. What vertically integrated element makes this structurally hard to replicate quickly?
2. What does a buyer with 10x the Tier 2 budget actually need — not more of the same, but what new infrastructure or leverage?
3. What is the white-glove version where the prospect does literally nothing?

Build with:

**Outcome statement:** Premium result. Specific number. Specific timeframe. Bigger than Tier 2.

**What is different from Tier 2:** Every element in Tier 3 that does not exist in Tier 2. If the only difference is more quantity, go back to 10x ideation.

**Vertical integration element:** The one thing that makes this offer structurally hard to copy.

**Price logic:** Why this price is rational — compare to assembling equivalent resources separately.

**Grand Slam check:** All five.

## STAGE 4 — Objection Map

Pull every proof point from the business/intake details. Map each one to a specific objection from the ICP psychographic input.

Build as a table:

| Objection | Risk Eliminator |
|-----------|----------------|
| "Will this work for us?" | [Specific case study or process proof] |
| "We have tried this before" | [Specific structural differentiator from past failed solutions in ICP Section 4] |
| "Too expensive" | [ROI calculation tied to Stage 1 cost of inaction] |
| "We handle this in-house" | [Why in-house costs more than it appears] |
| "We are not ready" | [What they are losing while they wait] |
| "I don't know what I'd be getting" | [Scope breakdown from Tier 1 or Tier 2] |

Also check for these AI-era objections and include any that apply:

| Objection | Risk Eliminator |
|-----------|----------------|
| "We can just use AI for this" | [Specific judgment call, irreversible decision, or accountability gap a tool cannot fill] |
| "How do I know this won't be outdated in 6 months?" | [Mechanism for staying current — revision cadence, update policy, built-in iteration] |
| "Everyone is doing this — how is this different?" | [One specific mechanism that produces a result AI cannot easily replicate] |
| "AI can do this faster and cheaper" | [What the buyer loses by going AI-only — the specific mistake or downstream cost] |

Remove any row that does not apply to this specific prospect. Every remaining row must have a specific eliminator, not a generic claim.

If proof points are missing: insert `[NEEDS: specific proof point description]` — do not fabricate.

## STAGE 5 — The Outcome Sentence

Write one sentence. This is the DNA of the entire document.

Format:
> "We help [specific prospect] achieve [specific outcome] in [timeframe] without [effort they hate most], guaranteed by [specific proof]."

Rules — enforce every one:

**Specific prospect:** Not "B2B companies." Name the exact buyer at the exact stage.

**Specific outcome:** Not the service. Not the category of the result. The actual before/after state.
- ❌ "brand clarity" → ✅ "a one-page document that tells every copywriter and agency exactly what your brand stands for, so you stop rewriting briefs"
- ❌ "better positioning" → ✅ "conversion rate moves from X% to Y% within Z days"
The outcome must contain a specific number, a specific deliverable with a specific use, or a specific before/after state. If it contains none of these, it is abstract. Rewrite it.

**Specific timeframe:** A real number. "Within 14 days." Not "fast."

**Effort removal:** The single task from Stage 2 (Effort lens) they would pay to never do again.

**Specific proof:** Named client plus result, or success rate. If none exists: `[NEEDS: proof point]`.

## DOCUMENT ASSEMBLY — Offer Clarity Document

### Formatting rules — enforce before writing a single word

- **Simple language first:** Write like you are explaining to a smart 12-year-old. Everyday words only. If a simpler word exists, use it — 'use' not 'utilize', 'show' not 'demonstrate', 'help' not 'facilitate', 'find' not 'identify'. Every sentence should be easy to read aloud in one breath.
- Maximum 20 words per sentence. Split anything longer.
- Maximum 3 sentences per paragraph. One idea per paragraph. Use bullets if more to say.
- One fact per bullet. Maximum 12 words per bullet.
- No throat-clearing: do not open any section with a sentence that restates the heading.
- No stacked adjectives: replace every adjective cluster with the fact it is hiding.
  - ❌ "comprehensive, battle-tested, proven process" → ✅ "a 5-step process used on 12 client engagements"
- Price logic = two lines max: alternative cost on one line, the math on the next.
- Viking check before finalizing: find every sentence over 20 words, every paragraph over 3 sentences, every bullet over 12 words. Rewrite each one.

## Anti-Patterns — Check Before Delivering

- **Service description as offer**: "We do X for Y" is not an offer. An offer states outcome, timeframe, effort removed, and proof.
- **Pricing without anchoring**: A price without a cost-of-inaction comparison means nothing.
- **Vague proof**: "We have a proven process" kills no objection.
- **Generic prospect descriptions**: "Growing companies" is not a prospect description.
- **Tiering by quantity**: Tier 2 is not "more of Tier 1." Each tier solves a different problem for a different buyer.
- **Abstract outcomes**: "Better positioning" is not an outcome. "Conversion rate moves from 1.2% to 2.4% within 60 days" is an outcome.
- **Effort removal that is effort transfer**: "We collaborate closely with your team" is not effort removal. If they still have to brief, review, or manage — say so honestly.
