# PMF Synthesis Engine
## Product-Market Fit Skill — Phase 3

This phase maps the ICP Psychographics Document against the Offer Clarity Document across four dimensions. It does not generate new analysis from scratch — it compares specific facts from both documents and finds where they match or don't.

Input: The complete Phase 1 (ICP) and Phase 2 (Offer) outputs from this session.

**Language rule:** Write like you are explaining to a smart 12-year-old. Use short sentences. Use everyday words. If a simpler word exists, use it — 'use' not 'utilize', 'show' not 'demonstrate', 'help' not 'facilitate'. Every sentence should be easy to read aloud in one breath.

---

## The Four Dimensions

Run every dimension. Do not skip any.

---

### Dimension 1 — Problem-Solution Fit

**What to compare:**
- **ICP source:** Section 2 (Problems & Frustrations) — the specific problems, in the ICP's own words, ranked by how bad they feel
- **Offer source:** Stage 1 (Cost of Inaction) — the problem the offer claims to solve and what it costs

**What to check:**
1. Does the offer address the #1 most painful ICP problem — the one rated High — directly and explicitly?
2. Does the language the offer uses to describe the problem match the language the ICP uses, or does the offer describe the problem differently?
3. Is the cost calculation in the offer based on what the ICP actually loses (from their specific frustrations), or is it a generic category estimate?
4. Are the "what happens if it doesn't get fixed" consequences from ICP Section 2 reflected anywhere in the offer?

**How to score:**

- **Strong Fit:** The offer directly addresses the top 1–2 ICP problems. The problem language matches or closely mirrors ICP Section 2. The cost of inaction uses ICP-specific details.
- **Partial Fit:** The offer addresses the right general area but misses the specific problem ranked highest. Language doesn't match — the offer describes the problem in seller terms, not buyer terms. Cost framing is general.
- **Gap:** The offer solves a problem the ICP has but doesn't rank as most painful. Or the offer solves a problem the ICP doesn't describe having at all.

---

### Dimension 2 — Desire-Outcome Fit

**What to compare:**
- **ICP source:** Section 3 (What They Want) — the specific results the ICP is chasing, how they measure success, and what life looks like when the problem is solved
- **Offer source:** Stage 5 (Outcome Sentence) + Tier 2 outcome statement — what the offer promises to deliver

**What to check:**
1. Does the offer's promised outcome match what the ICP says they want — or what the seller assumes they should want?
2. Does the offer's success metric match how the ICP measures success (from "How they know something is working")?
3. Does the "life after" picture in the Offer Positioning section match the ICP's description of "what their life looks like when the problem is solved"?
4. Is the timeframe in the outcome sentence reasonable relative to what the ICP is expecting?

**How to score:**

- **Strong Fit:** Offer outcome maps directly to ICP desired outcome. Same or compatible success metrics. The "life after" picture in both documents describes the same change.
- **Partial Fit:** Offer delivers a related outcome but not the one the ICP is actively chasing. Different success metric. Seller's vision of success ≠ buyer's vision of success.
- **Gap:** Offer promises something the ICP doesn't want or doesn't rank highly. Clear disconnect between what's sold and what's actually desired.

---

### Dimension 3 — Identity-Positioning Fit

**What to compare:**
- **ICP source:** Section 4 (What They Really Want) — specifically: "Who they want to be" (From/To identity shift), "How they want people to see them"
- **Offer source:** Positioning section — "This is for," "What makes this different," the framing of the Offer Summary

**What to check:**
1. Does the offer positioning speak to the identity shift (From → To), or does it only speak to the functional result?
2. Does the offer language use the ICP's desired self-concept — the version of themselves they want to become?
3. Is the "This is NOT for" statement accurate and specific — does it exclude people who would produce bad results, not just people without budget?
4. Does the Offer Summary's language give the buyer a way to see themselves differently, or does it only describe a transaction?

**How to score:**

- **Strong Fit:** Positioning names or implies the identity shift explicitly. Language mirrors how the ICP describes their desired self. "Not for" is specific and meaningful. Reading the offer makes the ICP think "that's describing me — the version I want to be."
- **Partial Fit:** Positioning gestures at identity but stays mostly functional. The emotional and status layers from ICP Section 4 are absent. The offer describes what you get, not who you become.
- **Gap:** Positioning is entirely transactional and functional. Makes no connection to who the buyer wants to become. Could apply to any buyer with the same problem regardless of what they want their identity to be.

---

### Dimension 4 — Fear-Objection Fit

**What to compare:**
- **ICP source:** Section 4 (What They Really Want) — specifically: "What they are scared of" (fears about trying and failing, what it says about them if they can't figure this out, what they've already tried)
- **Offer source:** Stage 4 (Objection Map) — the objections addressed and the specific eliminators

**What to check:**
1. Are the ICP's specific fears present in the objection map — the identity-level fears, not just the transactional ones?
2. Does the objection map address the fear of "what if I try this and it still doesn't work" — or only "will this work in general"?
3. Are the ICP's past failed solutions addressed specifically in the objection map — with a structural differentiator that explains why this is different, not just "we're better"?
4. Does the guarantee address the specific fear, or is it a generic satisfaction guarantee?

**How to score:**

- **Strong Fit:** Objection map addresses the ICP's specific fears in terms close to how the ICP expressed them. Past failures are addressed with a structural differentiator. The guarantee speaks directly to the fear of wasting money again.
- **Partial Fit:** Objection map covers transactional objections (price, timing, trust) but misses the emotional and identity-level fears. Guarantee is present but generic.
- **Gap:** Objection map is entirely transactional. The real fears — fear of looking incompetent, fear of being wrong again, fear of wasting money on something that won't work — are not addressed anywhere in the offer.

## PMF Score

After running all four dimensions, calculate the score:

Count the number of Strong Fit ratings. Multiply by 2.5 to get the score out of 10.

| Score | Label | What It Means |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| 10/10 | Ready | The offer speaks to the right people about the right problem with the right promise. Focus on getting it in front of buyers. |
| 7.5/10 | Mostly ready | One real gap. Fix it before you spend money on ads or cold outreach. The offer will work but you'll lose buyers at one clear point. |
| 5/10 | Needs work | Two gaps means buyers drop off at two different moments. Fix the worst gap first. Don't try to fix both at the same time. |
| 2.5/10 or 0/10 | Misaligned | Do not spend money on distribution yet. The offer is solving for something the ICP doesn't want, or is aimed at the wrong buyer. Go back to the problem framing. |

## Gap Analysis

For every dimension rated Partial Fit or Gap, write a gap entry in this format:

**Gap: [Dimension Name]**

**The mismatch:**
[One to two sentences. What the ICP says vs. what the offer says. Specific — quote or closely paraphrase both documents.]

**Why this matters:**
[One sentence. At what point in the buyer journey does this gap cause someone to disengage — when they read the headline, when they read the outcome statement, when they see the pricing, when they consider their past failures?]

**The fix:**
[Two to four sentences. Not a category of action — the exact sentence, section, or element to change. What to add, what to remove, what to rewrite. If a rewrite is needed, write the new version here.]

---

## Recommendations

After the gap analysis, write 3–5 prioritized recommendations. Order by impact — the fix that closes the most damaging gap goes first.

**Recommendation [N]: [Short descriptive name]**
- **What to change:** The specific element — a sentence, a tier structure, a proof point, a positioning statement, an objection row
- **Why it matters:** Which dimension this closes and what buyer behavior it changes as a result
- **How to do it:** Two to four sentences. Actionable. Specific. If possible, write the new version of the thing being changed.

---

## PMF Verdict

One paragraph. Five sentences. Plain English. No jargon.

Write it in this order:
1. What this product is and who it's for — one plain sentence in your own words, not the offer language
2. What the overall fit looks like: name the strongest dimension and the weakest one
3. The single most important thing to fix before spending money on distribution
4. Whether this product is ready to sell to this audience right now — a direct answer with no hedging
5. What to do next — one concrete action
